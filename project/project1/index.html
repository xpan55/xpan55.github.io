The influence of translation ambiguity on bilinguals' reading in L1

Investigating the influence of translation ambiguity on Chinese-English and French-English bilinguals' first language reading with an on-line self-paced reading task
on-line SPR translation ambiguity bilingual

This is a project I did with my supervisor Dr. Debra Jared at Western University and the RAs in our lab: Yae Ram Kim, Jia-Wei Shih, and Alexandra Sherwin.

Backgrounds

Translation ambiguity occurs when a word in one language has more than one translation in another language. For example, the English word bark means “dog bark” and “tree bark,” and they translate into distinct French words: aboie and écorce, respectively. It’s the same case in Chinese: 狗叫 for dog bark and 树皮 for tree bark. This mapping from one English word to two words in other languages is called translation ambiguity.

In a bilingual’s daily life, the two unrelated words (aboiement and ecorce) could be activated together because the shared association with the same English word bark. As a result of this concurrent activation, the association between aboiement and ecorce could be strengthened although they are unrelated in French.

In this project, we examined the influence of such translation ambiguity in bilinguals’ second language (L2, English) on their first language (L1) reading.

Method & Experiment

French-English and Chinese-English bilinguals read anormal sentences in their L1 in an online self-paced reading task. These anormal sentences were created based on the dominant meanings (e.g., dog bark) but correct translation (aboie) was replaced either by the translation of the other meaning (e.g., écorce) or by a spelling control (e.g., cerise).

Example anormal sentences in Frensh and Chinese:

  • Le chien a laissé échapper une écorce/cerise bruyante lorsqu’il a vu un écureuil.
  • 他的狗在看到松鼠时大声的树皮/柳木了一声。
  • (The dog let out a loud tree bark/spelling control when he saw a squirrel.)

Bilinguals read the sentences in an online moving window self-paced reading (SPR) task, in which they control their own pace of reading by pressing the spacebar. Data were collected on how long it takes for them to read each word in the sentence.

Restuls

Reading times for the translation ambiguity word, one word before, one word after, and two words after the ambiguity word were analyzed. Bilinguals spent less time reading the translation ambiguity error words than the controls. Differences in reading times were also observed one word and two words after the critical word.

Take home

Bilingual’s native language processing is influenced by translation ambiguity. Mapping of two L1 words onto one L2 word strengthens the associations between the two otherwise unrelated L1 words.
